From the air the shape is plain. A wooded promontory pushes south into Lake Como and divides it in two — the Como arm running southwest, the Lecco arm southeast — an inverted Y with Bellagio on the dividing point. The town holds the tip. Ferries work the quay below the pastel houses. Over the shallows the water runs clear and green, and the lake's mountains close the far shore. This was August 2023.

The split has a name: Punta Spartivento, the point that divides the wind. It sits at the promontory's northern tip, a ten-minute walk north of the main square. Stand there and the northern lake opens straight ahead, one long reach of water aimed at the pre-Alps, with the two southern arms falling away on either side. Boatmen have used the point as a wind-and-current marker for as long as the lake has been worked.

Bellagio doesn't sprawl; it climbs. Behind the waterfront the streets turn into salite — narrow stepped lanes of cobble and stone — and Salita Serbelloni runs from the lake to the upper town in one long staircase. The quay itself is a hinge in the lake's ferry network: the central-lake boats tie Bellagio to Varenna and Menaggio across the water, the three mid-lake towns trading passengers all day.

Two gardens hold the headland. Villa Melzi, built 1808 to 1810 in plain neoclassical stone for Francesco Melzi d'Eril — vice-president of Napoleon's Italian Republic — keeps an English-style garden along the shoreline. Above the town, the park of Villa Serbelloni covers almost the entire promontory, some 21 hectares of terraces and wooded paths over a villa first laid out around 1400. From overhead the green reads as the roof of the point.
